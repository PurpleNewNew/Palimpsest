## Structural rules

- The central idea atom should usually be a `hypothesis` atom expressing the proposed answer or mechanism.
- Downstream proposition atoms should usually be `claim` atoms (covering algorithmic / mechanistic / formal-analytical statements).
- Empirical-test atoms should usually be `finding` atoms.
- Use a `question` atom for the central idea only when the idea is genuinely an open inquiry rather than a proposed answer.
- Do not build a broad survey tree.
- Do not build a generic "operationalization family" node listing many unrelated possibilities.
- Prefer one concrete operationalization seed and build around it.
- The idea atom is the conceptual root.
- Claim atoms should make the idea actionable or analyzable.
- Finding atoms should test concrete downstream claim atoms, not the raw idea directly.

## Claim atom rules

Claim atoms must describe concrete candidate mechanisms, modules, training paradigms, or analytical statements that could induce the desired property.

Good mechanistic claim atoms:
- post-update singular value rebalance
- spectral entropy regularization during training
- low-rank update parameterization that discourages dominant singular directions
- projected update rule that preserves a flatter spectrum

Good analytical claim atoms:
- balanced spectra reduce dominant-direction overwrite
- flatter update spectra reduce repeated concentration on a few unstable directions

Bad claim atoms:
- operationalization family
- candidate objects list
- possible methods in general

Claim atoms may be:
- parallel alternatives
- or a short incremental chain where one claim extends another

## Finding atom rules

Every finding atom must describe a specific way to test one concrete claim atom, not just a research topic.

Each finding atom should answer:
- which upstream claim atom it is testing
- what is manipulated or compared
- what the baseline or control is
- what metrics or failure modes are observed
- what outcome would support or weaken the upstream claim and, indirectly, the central idea

Good finding atoms are concrete, for example:
- compare post-training singular-value rebalance against the original update
- compare balanced-spectrum noise against imbalanced-spectrum noise at matched norm
- compare balanced vs unbalanced updates under the same parameter budget

Bad finding atoms are too vague, for example:
- observational study
- intervention benchmark
- validation branch

## Relation rules

- Follow the relation semantics from `research.txt` strictly.
- The idea atom should usually connect to downstream claim atoms with `motivates`.
- The idea atom may also connect to claim atoms with `formalizes` or `analyzes`, depending on whether the downstream node is a proposal, formalization, or analysis.
- Claim atoms should connect to finding atoms with `validates`.
- Keep the tree readable and sparse.

Never reverse `validates`.
- Do not create `finding -> claim`
- Do not create `finding -> hypothesis`
- Do not create `finding -> idea`

Direction principle:
- source is conceptually prior or more foundational
- target is downstream, derived, analyzed, or empirically evaluated

## Atom quality rules

- One atom = one main claim + its direct evidence.
- Do not mix claim and evidence in the same field.
- Do not put an `Evidence` section inside `claim`.
- Do not put a `Claim` section inside `evidence`.
- Prefer standard markdown math compatible with KaTeX: `$...$` and `$$...$$`.
